In Conversation: Rising Stars of the Underground

A deep dive with three artists shaping the future of techno

The underground techno scene is constantly evolving, driven by artists who refuse to compromise their vision for commercial success. Today, I'm sitting down with three producers who represent the cutting edge of this movement: Sarah Vex from Berlin, Marco Luna from Barcelona, and Yuki Tanaka from Tokyo.

What started as separate conversations in green rooms and after-parties across Europe has evolved into this collaborative interview. These three artists have never met in person, but their music speaks the same language – raw, uncompromising, and deeply rooted in underground culture.

Meet the Artists

Sarah Vex

Berlin, Germany

Industrial techno producer and founder of Concrete Void Records. Known for her hardware-only live sets and uncompromising sound design.

SoundCloud Bandcamp Instagram

Marco Luna

Barcelona, Spain

Deep techno specialist with releases on Semantica and Mord. His sound blends hypnotic grooves with experimental sound design.

SoundCloud Resident Advisor Instagram

Yuki Tanaka

Tokyo, Japan

Ambient techno producer bridging the gap between noise music and dancefloor functionality. Resident at Tokyo's legendary Contact club.

Bandcamp SoundCloud Instagram

The Conversation

Q

Let's start with the obvious – what drew each of you to the underground techno scene?

Sarah Vex

For me, it was about finding a space where experimentation was not just accepted but encouraged. I grew up on industrial and noise music, and when I discovered the Berlin techno scene, I realized there was a place where those influences could exist on the dancefloor.

The underground isn't just about music – it's about community, about rejecting the mainstream's obsession with accessibility and polish. Raw is beautiful.

Marco Luna

I came from a jazz background, believe it or not. What attracted me to underground techno was the emphasis on improvisation and the spiritual aspect of extended DJ sets. In Barcelona's after-hours scene, I found DJs playing 8, 10, even 12-hour sets – that's not just DJing, that's storytelling.

Yuki Tanaka

In Tokyo, the underground is quite different from Europe. There's less emphasis on the "dark" aesthetic and more on texture and atmosphere. I was drawn to artists like Donato Dozzy and Voices from the Lake who proved techno could be meditative and psychedelic, not just aggressive.

Q

The underground scene has always been about more than just the music. How do you navigate the tension between staying true to your sound and reaching a wider audience?

Marco Luna

That's the eternal question, isn't it? I think the key is not chasing the audience – let them find you. I've turned down remix opportunities and label deals that would have given me more exposure because they wanted me to "make it more accessible." That's code for "water it down."

Sarah Vex

I don't see it as a tension at all. My audience will always be niche, and I'm completely fine with that. I'd rather play to 200 people who truly understand what I'm doing than 2,000 people who are just there for the Instagram moment.

The underground survives because we refuse to compromise. The moment you start thinking about "reaching a wider audience," you've already lost what made your sound special.

"The underground survives because we refuse to compromise."

— Sarah Vex
Yuki Tanaka

I have a slightly different perspective. I think you can grow your audience without compromising, but it requires patience. My Bandcamp following has grown steadily over five years, not through promotion or marketing, but through consistent output and word of mouth.

The algorithm-driven world tries to tell us we need to be everywhere all the time. We don't. Quality over quantity applies to audience building too.

Q

Let's talk about production. What's your creative process like? Do you have a specific workflow or does it change from track to track?

Yuki Tanaka

I'm very much a "vibe" producer. I never sit down with a plan. I usually start with field recordings or found sounds – last week I recorded the sounds of the Tokyo subway at 2 AM and built an entire track around that texture.

My studio is minimal: a laptop, one synth (currently a Make Noise 0-Coast), and a handful of effects pedals. Limitations breed creativity.

Sarah Vex

I'm the opposite – I'm all hardware, all the time. Modular synths, drum machines, effects units. No computer except for recording the final mix. This approach forces me to commit to decisions in real-time, which I think gives my music a more organic feel.

My process is almost like a live performance. I'll jam for an hour, record everything, then go back and find the moments that worked.

Marco Luna

I'm somewhere in between. I use a hybrid setup – Ableton for arrangement and some software synths, but I run everything through analog gear for warmth and character. My secret weapon is an old Eventide H3000 that I found at a flea market in Valencia.

I usually start with the groove – get the kick and bass locked in perfectly, then build everything around that foundation.

Q

The pandemic fundamentally changed the club scene. How has that affected your approach to making music?

Marco Luna

It was devastating at first. I make music for clubs, and suddenly there were no clubs. But it forced me to think differently about my sound. I started making tracks that worked in headphones, not just on a massive sound system.

That exploration led to my "Lockdown Sessions" EP, which is some of my favorite work. Silver linings, I guess.

"Silence taught me more about production than any tutorial ever could."

— Yuki Tanaka
Yuki Tanaka

The pandemic silence was profound. Tokyo went from one of the loudest cities on earth to eerily quiet. That silence taught me more about production than any tutorial ever could. I learned to appreciate space, negative space, in a way I never had before.

Now, even though clubs are back, that influence remains in my music. Less is often more.

Sarah Vex

Honestly, it made me more militant about the importance of underground spaces. Seeing how quickly governments shut down clubs while leaving other businesses open made it clear – we're not valued. We're seen as dispensable.

That realization fueled my music. My post-pandemic releases are angrier, more confrontational. I'm done being polite.

Q

Where do you see the underground techno scene heading in the next few years?

Sarah Vex

I think we're going to see a split. On one hand, the "underground" aesthetic will continue to be commodified and watered down for festivals. On the other hand, there will always be true underground spaces and artists who reject that commercialization.

The real underground will go even deeper underground, if that makes sense.

Marco Luna

I'm optimistic. I see younger producers pushing boundaries in exciting ways. The tools are more accessible than ever, which means more voices, more perspectives. Yes, that means more noise to cut through, but the gems are still there.

Yuki Tanaka

I think geography matters less and less. The internet has created these global micro-scenes where artists from Tokyo, Berlin, São Paulo, and Johannesburg are influencing each other in real-time. That cross-pollination is exciting.

The future of underground techno is global, collaborative, and impossible to categorize. And that's exactly how it should be.

Q

Last question – any advice for producers just starting out who want to break into the underground scene?

Yuki Tanaka

Stop trying to "break in." Just make music that you love and share it. The right people will find it. Focus on the craft, not the career.

Marco Luna

Don't imitate. Too many producers try to sound like their heroes. Influence is fine, but you need to find your own voice. What makes YOUR music necessary?

Sarah Vex

Be patient. I've been producing for 8 years and I'm just now getting regular bookings outside Berlin. This isn't a sprint. Also, go to clubs. Support the scene. You can't be part of a community if you're not participating in it.

And most importantly: stay weird. The world has enough generic techno.

Final Thoughts

Conversations like this remind me why I love this scene. Despite being separated by thousands of miles and coming from different musical backgrounds, these three artists share a common thread: an uncompromising dedication to their craft and a refusal to let commercial pressures dictate their creative direction.

The underground isn't a place or even a sound – it's a mindset. It's about valuing authenticity over popularity, community over clout, and artistry over algorithm.

Thanks to Sarah, Marco, and Yuki for taking the time to share their perspectives. Follow them, support them, and most importantly – go see them play if they come to your city.
